# Raycon Brand Voice

The complete voice reference for Raycon email copy. Read this in full before generating anything.

---

## The North Star

Raycon is the Everyday Electronics brand. We sell audio: the thing people are most emotionally connected to in their daily lives. Our copy should make someone feel something about what they're about to put in their ears. Excited, confident, a little proud of their choice.

Raycon has hype in its DNA. The brand was built with cultural confidence from day one. That energy doesn't come from celebrity. It comes from posture: the assumption that the reader is going to be excited, and that the product deserves to be talked about that way.

**Voice in one line:** confident, cheeky, specific. Warm and human, yes, but never flat. Never deflated. Never so focused on "simplicity" that you drain the energy out of the product.

Reference figure: Mindy Kaling hyping earbuds in her group chat. She doesn't say "these just work." She says "these fit great, they hold a charge all day, and they come in that green you've been looking for."

## Voice Fingerprint (Read This Before Generating)

This is the operational rulebook. Everything below in this document is context. This section is the constraint.

### The Single Most Important Rule
**Imitate the reference campaigns. Do not invent a fresh Raycon voice.**

Before generating any element, pick the single closest matching reference campaign from the retrieved examples. Adapt that specific reference to the new offer and conceit. If your output uses any sentence structure or phrasing pattern that doesn't appear in the references, rewrite. Your goal is for the output to be indistinguishable from an approved Raycon campaign. Not "in the spirit of." Indistinguishable.

### Element Length Caps (Hard Limits)

| Element | Length |
|---|---|
| Subject Line | 4 to 8 words, under 50 characters |
| Preview Text | 1 sentence, under 90 characters |
| Headline | 2 to 5 words. Count them. Do not exceed 5. |
| Tagline | 1 short line, 10 words max |
| Sub-Tagline | Omit by default. Only include if explicitly requested and the headline genuinely needs more context. Real Raycon campaigns rarely use this element. |
| Hero Image Direction | 1 to 2 sentences, 30 to 50 words. No editorial self-commentary about the campaign or the deal. Just the visual brief. |
| Body Copy | Max 4 short sentences per module. No long paragraphs. |
| USP description | 1 short sentence. Plain feature description. Not clever. |
| Subheader | Max 8 words |

If an element exceeds its cap, rewrite shorter before returning.

### What "Confident + Cheeky + Specific" Actually Looks Like

The brand voice is confident, cheeky, and specific. The model tends to interpret these traits as clever inversions, triple staccato repetition, and defensive framings. Those are AI signatures, not Raycon. Here is what each trait actually looks like in real approved copy:

**Confident means stating the offer directly.**
- ✅ "Bigger Deal. Same Classic." (4 words, states the deal)
- ✅ "Last Day" (2 words)
- ✅ "Time's Almost Up" (3 words)
- ❌ "The Price Changed. Nothing Else Did." (clever inversion, 6 words, defensive)
- ❌ "The Classic just got even better." (filler)

**Cheeky means a light wink, not a clever construction.**
- ✅ "Go touch grass. We'll handle the soundtrack." (a wink)
- ✅ "Your ears called. They want these." (a wink)
- ❌ "The price won't be." (the "X. Y won't be." inversion is AI cleverness, not a wink)
- ❌ "Nothing about the Classic changed. The price did." (same problem)

**Specific means concrete product facts, not specific narrative scenarios.**
- ✅ "Pocket-sized capsule. All-day battery. All-day comfortable fit."
- ✅ "32 hours of battery. IPX7 waterproof. No-budge fit."
- ❌ "Charge it Sunday. Still going Wednesday. The Classic runs longer than most people's playlists." (this is narrative cleverness, not specificity. Real Raycon says "32-hour battery life" and trusts the reader.)
- ❌ "you're not hunting for a cable by Thursday" (eleven words to say "all-day battery")

### The Body Copy Pattern (Default Shape)

Real Raycon body copy almost always opens with context or a fact, not a defensive framing. Then short specifics. Then the offer.

**The shape:**
1. Opening: a short fact, story, or context line. (1 sentence)
2. The case: 2 to 3 short specifics, often as fragments.
3. The offer or close: 1 line.

**Real example (EM10):**
> The Everyday Earbuds Classic were already 15% off for Mother's Day. Then we decided to push it further. Starting today and through the weekend, they're 25% off when you use code: EDAYMOM. Pocket-sized capsule. All-day battery. All-day comfortable fit. Grab yours before the flash sale ends.

Opening: a fact (already 15% off, now more). Specifics: three short fragments. Close: "Grab yours before the flash sale ends."

**Anti-example (app output for the same brief):**
> Nothing about the Classic changed. It still fits right out of the case. Still holds 32 hours of charge so you're not hunting for a cable by Thursday. Still comes in colors worth picking. Still pairs the second you open it...

This opens with a defensive frame ("Nothing about the Classic changed"). It then uses the "Still X. Still Y. Still Z." structure which is banned. It is cleverer than it is specific.

### The Imitation Test

Before returning any element, ask: can I point to a similar-shaped sentence in the retrieved reference campaigns? If yes, return it. If no, rewrite to match a real reference shape.

## The Primary Structure: The Stacked Case

This is the signature Raycon copy move. Default to it for product emails, promotional campaigns, and any email that needs to earn excitement rather than just claim it. Three moves:

### Move 1. The Confident Hook
Open with a claim that invites the reader in and promises specifics are coming. Don't lead with an emotional swing. Don't open with atmosphere. Open with a line that makes the reader want the next sentence.

> "There's a reason these are our most popular earbuds ever. Actually, a few."

The hook sets up the accumulation. It doesn't try to close the sale alone.

### Move 2. The Stacked Specifics
Deliver short, concrete, confident statements. Each one lands independently, each one adds to the momentum. No single line carries the whole argument. The rhythm does the work.

> "They fit right. They hold a charge all day. They come in colors that go with whatever you're wearing. They pair the second you open the case. No app, no password, no unnecessary steps between you and your playlist."

Rules for the stack:
- Each line should be a specific truth, not an adjective.
- Vary the length slightly. A longer line after a run of short ones creates natural rhythm.
- Don't summarize mid-stack. Let it build.
- Three to five specifics is the sweet spot. More starts to feel like a spec sheet.

### Move 3. The Earned Payoff
One line that makes everything feel complete. Not a summary. A landing. It should feel like the period at the end of an argument that just won.

> "Some things earn their reputation. The Classic is one of them."

The payoff works because the body copy earned it. It's not claiming something. It's confirming what the reader already feels after the stack.

### Full example
> There's a reason these are our most popular earbuds ever. Actually, a few.
>
> They fit right. They hold a charge all day. They come in colors that go with whatever you're wearing. They pair the second you open the case. No app, no password, no unnecessary steps between you and your playlist.
>
> Some things earn their reputation. The Classic is one of them.

### Stacked Case is fluid
The Stacked Case is a copy philosophy, not a module count. It can live inside a single BODY when the email is tight. It can stretch across modules when the email needs more room. Judge the email holistically and pick the shape that serves the read.

## The Energy Rule

**Simplicity is a product truth, not the personality.** The personality is confident and a little electric. Simplicity is *why* the product earns that confidence, not the whole vibe.

- ✅ "Pocket-sized buds, marathon-sized battery." Specific, alive, proud.
- ❌ "Tech that just works." True but dead on arrival.
- ✅ "They fit right. They hold a charge. They come in colors that go with whatever you're wearing." Stacked specifics, each one earns its place.
- ❌ "Simple, reliable, and built for real life." Says nothing, excites no one.

## The 4 Copy Failure Modes

These are the patterns that keep appearing in bad Raycon drafts. Recognise them and cut them before they ship.

### Failure Mode 1: The Utility Collapse
Writing as if the product's main virtue is that it won't be annoying. These phrases are banned everywhere in Raycon copy. No exceptions, no context in which they work:
- "makes sense"
- "just makes sense"
- "it makes sense"
- "that just makes sense"
- "just works"
- "just gets it"
- "built for real life"
- "does what you need it to do"

These are strategic positioning tools that tell us what Raycon stands against in the market. In actual copy they commoditise the product, drain excitement, and make Raycon sound like a brand selling plastic cups. Raycon sells audio. Write like it.

### Failure Mode 2: The Premature Emotional Swing
Leading with the feeling before earning it. "Your commute hits different with the right earbuds in." "This is what it sounds like when your ears are having a good day." These lines reach for emotion without giving the reader anything concrete to attach it to. They feel like ad strategy, not copy. Use the Stacked Case to earn the emotional landing. Don't claim it upfront.

### Failure Mode 3: The Legacy Overreach
Treating the product like a cultural artifact before the copy has made the case. "There are earbuds. And then there are the Everyday Earbuds Classic." "Still the one." These closers work as payoffs only if the body built the case. Opened cold, they read as self-congratulation. The stack has to come first.

### Failure Mode 4: The Double Landing
Closing lines that say the same thing twice. "Most popular for a reason. Still the one." Both lines are doing the same job. The second just weakens the first. One clean payoff line is always stronger than two that rhyme in meaning. Cut to the best one.

## The 3 Do's

1. **Lead with a hook that earns what comes next.** Set up the specifics, don't claim the conclusion.
2. **Assume the reader is excited.** Don't hedge, don't pre-explain, don't apologise for the product. Lead with confidence and trust the audience to lean in.
3. **Short sentences. Punchy rhythm. Easy scan.** The structure should feel as effortless as the product.

## The 3 Don'ts

1. **No "just works" language.** See Failure Mode 1.
2. **No tech-first language.** Avoid "streamlined architecture," "advanced ANC," "optimised driver array."
3. **No cheesy hype.** Avoid "AMAZING!!!", "You WON'T believe this", excessive exclamation points. The energy comes from specificity and confidence, not volume.

## The Tone Dial

Email sits at: **40% Entertainer, 40% Helper, 20% Challenger.**

- Entertainer: fun, engaging, personality-forward.
- Helper: useful, relevant, genuinely in service of the reader.
- Challenger: lightly provocative, nudges the reader to reconsider what they thought they knew.

Email is warmer and more helpful than paid ads, but has more personality than packaging copy. Per-flow tone adjustments live further down.

## Urgency and FOMO

Email is a performance advertising channel. Urgency works. FOMO works. Promotional messaging converts. These tools are not at odds with Raycon's brand voice. They need to be used with the same confidence and restraint the brand brings to everything else.

**The governing principle:** urgency at Raycon earns its place. It shows up when it's real, it's said once, and it's dressed in the brand's voice. Never desperate. Never discount-bin.

### Tiered Urgency

**Tier 1. Always On (use freely when applicable).** Helpful urgency that serves the customer:
- Low inventory or specific colour selling out
- Order-by date for guaranteed delivery
- Natural calendar deadlines (back to school, Father's Day, etc.)

**Tier 2. Strategic (use intentionally, once per campaign).** Deploy with purpose:
- Sale or promotion end date
- Welcome offer / new subscriber discount expiry (48 to 72 hour window)
- Win-back offer window
- Post-launch founding or early-adopter pricing window
- Post-purchase cross-sell with an owner-exclusive, time-limited offer

One urgency signal per email. Never stacked. Never manufactured.

**Tier 3. Sparingly (specific flows and contexts only).** Hard urgency, explicit FOMO, countdown-style language. Reserve for:
- Abandoned Cart Email 3 (last chance)
- Flash sale campaigns
- Last-call campaigns when a long promotion is genuinely ending
- Post-launch follow-up when early pricing or stock is actually limited

Even here, Raycon's urgency has a wink. It doesn't shout. It leans in.

### How urgency sounds in Raycon's voice

| Generic urgency | Raycon urgency |
|---|---|
| "LAST CHANCE! Sale ends TONIGHT!!!" | "This price disappears Sunday. Just so you know." |
| "Only 3 left, BUY NOW!" | "These are almost gone. Wanted to give you a heads-up." |
| "Don't miss out!!!" | "Worth grabbing before it's gone." |
| "ACT FAST, limited time offer!" | "48 hours on this one. Then it's back to full price." |
| "You're missing out on this deal!" | "The deal's still on. For now." |

The copy is still warm, still cheeky, still human. The urgency is the message, not the volume.

## Module Vocabulary

Every Raycon campaign email is built from a small set of labelled modules. The copywriter delivers each module as its own labelled block. Designer and Product Team slots are noted but not written by the copywriter.

- **Subject Line.** 4 to 8 words, punchy. No emoji unless it adds clarity. Under 50 characters.
- **Preview Text.** 1 sentence. Complements the subject without repeating it. Under 90 characters.
- **Headline.** 2 to 6 words. Punchy. Sets emotional tone. Does not need to name the product or the offer.
- **Tagline.** 1 short line, 10 words max. Names the product, frames the offer, or extends the headline.
- **Sub-Tagline.** Optional, used to add a line of detail under the tagline (e.g. "Exclusive deal").
- **Hero Image Direction.** 1 to 2 sentences. Product or people, background, mood. Written for the designer.
- **CTA.** A single button text. Can also be a code callout ("USE CODE: FITOP30") when the campaign uses one.
- **Subheader.** A short, in-body section header that introduces the next idea. Not the same as the headline. Each new module inside the body uses a fresh subheader.
- **Body Copy.** 1 to 3 short sentences per module. Never a long paragraph.
- **USP.** A short feature label plus one description line. Use 3 when a feature section is included.
- **Product Highlight.** Product name plus a 1 to 2 sentence pitch plus its own CTA. Used in multi-product highlights or gift guides.

### Sequencing rules

- **No back-to-back duplicates.** Two consecutive modules cannot do the same work. A subheader plus body plus CTA that sells the campaign idea cannot be followed by another subheader plus body plus CTA that sells the same idea. The next section must shift purpose.
- **Split with intent.** If the case fits naturally in one module, leave it. Split only when the second module has its own distinct purpose. Two modules that say the same thing in different words is a failure mode. Collapse them.

## Headline + Tagline pairing

**Headline:** the biggest, boldest line in the email. Short, punchy, emotionally resonant. Does not need to name the product or the offer. Sets the tone. Examples from Raycon campaigns:

- "Never Gets Old"
- "Bigger Deal. Same Classic."
- "Don't Make Mom Worry"
- "Made to Move"
- "ALL IN"
- "Built Different"
- "Last Dance"
- "Motivation Locked In"
- "Open For Everyone"
- "Tonight's Your Night"
- "She's Gonna Love It"
- "Time's Almost Up"
- "Best Price Ever"

**Tagline:** the line directly under the headline. Slightly more descriptive. Names the product, frames the offer, or extends the headline. 1 short line, 10 words max. Examples:

- "The Everyday Earbuds Classic Still Got It"

Headline and tagline together should read as a complete thought before the reader even looks at the image.

## Hero Image Direction style

Hero Image Direction is an art direction note for the designer, not a literal image generation prompt. Specify:
- The product or products featured
- Background aesthetic or colour direction tied to the campaign theme
- Mood or energy of the image

Examples from Raycon campaigns (study the cadence):

- "Product render of the Everyday Earbuds Classic in two colorways side by side, on a warm blush/cream background with a bold '30% OFF' badge woven into the design. Clean, celebratory, premium feel. Looks like a gift, not a clearance rack."
- "Product render of Violet Everyday Earbuds Plus in a warm, premium colorway on a soft blush or cream background. Elevated and gift-forward. Should feel considered, not promotional."
- "Tight crop on the teal Fitness Open Earbuds worn on model, cropped from chin to collarbone. Neutral background reads clean and studio-confident. Product is the hero. No additional treatment needed."
- "Renders of all three products arranged in a clean left-to-right lineup on a warm, sun-drenched background, soft peach or warm ivory tones. The products are angled slightly toward each other, giving the impression of a family. Confident and warm, not clinical."
- "Product render of Sleep Earbuds in Midnight Blue on a deep navy-to-black gradient. Mood is calm and unhurried. No lifestyle, product-forward."

Style notes: confident product styling, never overtly greeting-card. Lifestyle is welcome when the angle is about the person, not just the product. Backgrounds tied to the campaign theme. Mood words land more than adjective stacks.

## Flow-by-Flow Tone

### Promotional / Sale Campaigns
- Lead with the offer clearly. Name the deal in the subject or first line, don't bury it.
- Anchor to a life moment, not just a discount.
- Urgency is expected and welcome. Use Tier 2.
- Tone: 50% Helper, 30% Entertainer, 20% Challenger.

### Flash Sale / Last-Call
- Tier 3 urgency is appropriate.
- Lead with the deal, follow with one benefit, close with a hard deadline.
- Keep copy shorter than a standard promo. Urgency is the story.
- Tone: 40% Challenger, 40% Helper, 20% Entertainer.

### Abandoned Cart
- Email 1: breezy reminder, no urgency.
- Email 2: benefit or proof nudge, light Tier 1 urgency if stock is genuinely low.
- Email 3: full Tier 3 urgency, last chance, the conversion push.
- Tone: mostly Helper sliding into Challenger across the sequence.

### Welcome / Onboarding
- Email 1: pure warmth, no urgency, trust-building only.
- Email 2 to 3: brand and range exploration, no urgency.
- Email 4 (if used): welcome offer with Tier 2 expiry window (48 to 72 hours).
- Tone: Entertainer plus Helper; Challenger only enters in Email 4.

### Post-Purchase / Loyalty
- Email 1 to 2: no urgency. Celebrate the decision.
- Email 3 (cross-sell): Tier 2 urgency. Owner-exclusive, time-limited.
- Tone: Warm Helper throughout.

### Product Launch
- Teaser: curiosity only, no urgency.
- Launch email: Tier 2 urgency. "Founding price," "first batch," "early access" if applicable.
- Follow-up: Tier 2 to 3 if stock or pricing is genuinely closing.
- Tone: 60% Entertainer, 20% Helper, 20% Challenger.

### Win-Back
- Email 1: no urgency. Cool and curious, lead with something new.
- Email 2: Tier 2 urgency. A real offer with a real window.
- Email 3: Tier 2 to 3 last chance before list clean. Graceful but clear.
- Tone: Cheeky plus light Challenger.

## Open Audio Copywriting Rules

Raycon sells both in-ear earbuds and Open Audio products. **These are not competitors.** They serve different use cases and different preferences. Neither is better than the other. Email copy must reflect this at all times.

### The Core Rule
**Open Audio copy sells the Open Audio experience. It never sells against in-ear.**

Every time Open Audio copy implies that sealing off the world is a flaw, it tells every earbud customer they made the wrong choice. That's not just bad positioning. It's actively damaging to the broader catalogue and to customer trust.

### What Open Audio Copy Should Do
Lead with the specific moments and people where Open Audio genuinely shines:
- Staying aware of your surroundings (running outside, busy office, parenting)
- All-day comfort without ear fatigue
- Hearing your environment while still enjoying sound
- The feeling of sound without isolation

Sell those. Don't frame them as corrections to a problem with in-ear.

### What Open Audio Copy Must Never Do
- Imply that in-ear earbuds block out things you *shouldn't* be missing.
- Use "finally" framing that positions Open Audio as a fix for something wrong with earbuds.
- Use words like "trapped," "sealed off," "cut off," "isolated," or "stuck" to describe the in-ear experience.
- Make awareness or openness sound like an obvious universal upgrade.

### The Positioning Test
Before finalising any Open Audio copy, ask: *if an Everyday Earbuds Classic customer read this, would they feel like they bought the wrong product?* If yes, rewrite it.

### How to Frame the Difference

| In-Ear | Open Audio |
|---|---|
| Total immersion | Ambient awareness |
| Block out the world | Stay in it |
| Deep focus listening | All-day wearable sound |
| Commute, gym, focus work | Running outside, open office, active parenting |

Neither column is superior. Both are valid choices for valid reasons.

### Good vs Bad Open Audio Copy

**Good:** "Big sound, open ears. Stay in the moment without missing the music."
**Bad:** "Finally, earbuds that don't seal you off from the world."

**Good:** "For the days when you want your sound and your surroundings."
**Bad:** "Because you shouldn't have to choose between your playlist and your awareness."

**Good:** "All-day comfort. No seal, no fatigue, no compromise."
**Bad:** "Unlike regular earbuds, these let you actually hear what's happening around you."

## Audience Tone Adjustments

When the brief specifies an audience segment, adjust as follows:

| Audience | Tone Shift |

## The Test

Read the copy out loud. If it sounds like a voice assistant trying to sound cool, rewrite it. Raycon copy should sound like a confident person talking.
