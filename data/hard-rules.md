# Raycon Hard Rules: Do Not Do

These are absolute bans. They override creative judgment. The generator must check every output against this list before returning it.

---

## Punctuation and Formatting

- **No em dashes (—) anywhere.** The single most identifiable AI tell. Use a period or rewrite the sentence. No exceptions.
- **No ellipses for trailing effect.** "Sound that goes all day..." reads as AI performing wistfulness. Cut it.
- **No colon-as-reveal construction.** "One word: quality." / "The verdict: worth it." Overused AI device. Rewrite as a direct statement.
- **No all-caps words for emphasis** inside body copy. Headlines and CTAs may use full caps where appropriate (e.g. "USE CODE: MOTHER"), but mid-sentence shouting is banned.
- **Max one exclamation point per email.** Use sparingly. Reserve for genuine moments.
- **Subject lines under 50 characters.**
- **Preview text under 90 characters.**

---

## Banned Phrases (Failure Mode 1: Utility Collapse)

These commoditise the product. Never use them in Raycon copy:

- "makes sense"
- "just makes sense"
- "it makes sense"
- "that just makes sense"
- "just works"
- "just gets it"
- "built for real life"
- "does what you need it to do"
- "tech that just works"

---

## Banned AI-Tell Phrases

These read as AI-generated and erode trust:

- "elevate your experience"
- "elevate" (in any product or marketing context)
- "next-level"
- "game-changer"
- "game changing"
- "revolutionary"
- "we're excited to announce"
- "we're thrilled to introduce"
- "introducing" (as a campaign opener)
- "behold"
- "look no further"
- "unleash"
- "unlock the power of"
- "take it to the next level"

## Banned Structural Patterns (AI Stylisation)

- **No parenthetical personality asides.** "(yes, really)" / "(and we mean it)" / "(trust us)" / "(seriously)". AI deploys these to simulate warmth. They have the opposite effect on savvy readers. The personal-voice campaign template is an exception, but those asides must come from a real person's voice, not as warmth-fakery.
- **No fragment pairs or triple staccato with parallel shapes.** This ban covers every count and every position — headlines, subject lines, body copy, closing lines, everywhere. Two or three short fragments in a row with matching shape is the single most common AI writing rhythm:
  - 2-item pairs: "Real people. Real reviews." / "Big sale. Real savings." / "Real dads. Real reviews." Forbidden.
  - 3-item runs: "Real people. Real reviews. 20% off." / "Strong. Stylish. And built to last." Forbidden.
  If two or more consecutive fragments share the same grammatical shape, rewrite so the structures differ.
- **No "Adjective Noun. Adjective Noun." anaphora.** "Real people, real reviews." / "Big sound, bigger savings." / "Real dads, real reviews." The repeating Adjective + Noun shape is the most instantly recognisable AI headline pattern. It looks crafted, it reads as machine-written. Forbidden in every element.
- **No rhetorical question openers.** "Ever wish your earbuds just stayed put?" AI defaults to this. Open with a statement or a confident claim.
- **No "That's not X, that's Y" or "It's not X, it's Y" construction.** "That's not a coincidence, that's the Classic." / "It's not a sale. It's a statement." / "It's not just earbuds. It's your soundtrack." All variants of this inversion pattern are banned — the two-word pivot is AI cleverness, not Raycon voice. State the thing directly instead.
- **No "Same X. Same Y. Same Z." sequences.** "Same fit. Same battery. Same Classic." This is the triple staccato wearing a different costume. Rewrite as a single confident statement or fold the specifics into a stacked body.
- **No "Still X. Still Y. Still Z. Still W." sequences.** "Still fits right. Still holds a charge. Still comes in colors." Three or four consecutive sentences starting with the same word is an AI rhythm tell. Vary the openings.
- **No clever inversions in headlines or closes.** Examples to never write:
  - "The Price Changed. Nothing Else Did."
  - "The Classic is the same. The price won't be."
  - "Everything changed. Except this."
  - "The X. The Y won't be."
  These read as AI-clever. State the offer directly instead. ("Bigger Deal. Same Classic." is fine because it owns the offer; "The Price Changed. Nothing Else Did." is the inversion pattern wearing a Raycon mask.)
- **No defensive framings.** "The deal is real." "Nothing about the Classic changed." "We mean this." "This is not a drill." Real Raycon never defends. State the offer. State the proof. Move on.
- **No editorial self-commentary in Hero Image Direction.** Hero Image Direction is a visual brief for the designer, not a meta-commentary on the campaign strategy. Do not write phrases like:
  - "This is not a clearance situation."
  - "Feels like a product that earned a good week, not one that needed a markdown to move."
  - "It should look like something worth owning at full price, not something that needed a reason to sell."
  Just describe the visual: product, background, mood. 30 to 50 words. Done.
- **No narrative cleverness in USP descriptions.** USP descriptions are plain feature support. One short sentence. "32 hours of battery life." "Multi-Angular hook that won't budge." Not "Charge it Sunday. Still going Wednesday. The Classic runs longer than most people's playlists." Save the narrative for body copy.

## Banned Tech-First Language

Raycon doesn't lead with specs. Avoid:

- "streamlined architecture"
- "advanced ANC" (when ANC is the lead, not a supporting feature)
- "optimised driver array"
- "engineered for performance"
- "cutting-edge technology"
- "state-of-the-art"

Specs can support a claim. Specs cannot lead.

---

## Banned Cheesy Hype

- "AMAZING!!!"
- "You WON'T believe this"
- "Live out loud!!!!"
- "We're passionate about [anything]"
- "Colorful design with streamlined software architecture"
- "Mind-blowing"
- Multiple exclamation points in a row

## Semantic Contamination

**Never use a negative word in the same sentence as a product name or product reference, even to negate it.**

The reader's brain registers the word regardless of the negation.

❌ "The Classic isn't popular because it's cheap." (Reader now associates Classic with cheap.)
❌ "These don't sound tinny like other earbuds." (Reader now associates these with tinny.)
❌ "Won't fall out during your workout." (Reader now imagines them falling out.)

✅ "The Classic is popular because it delivers."
✅ "Sound that holds up in any environment."
✅ "Stays put, no matter what you're doing."

Make the positive case. Never defend against the negative one. If you're negating something, rewrite it as an affirmative claim.

---

## Open Audio vs In-Ear

Raycon sells both. Neither is better. Open Audio copy must never sell against in-ear.

- ❌ "Finally, earbuds that don't seal you off from the world."
- ❌ "Unlike regular earbuds, these let you actually hear what's happening around you."
- ❌ Words like "trapped," "sealed off," "cut off," "isolated," "stuck" applied to in-ear.
- ❌ "Finally" framing of any kind that positions Open Audio as a fix for earbuds.

The positioning test: if an Everyday Earbuds Classic customer read this, would they feel like they bought the wrong product? If yes, rewrite.

---

## Urgency Rules

- **No stacked urgency.** Never combine multiple urgency signals in one email ("Only 3 left AND sale ends tonight AND free shipping ends at midnight").
- **No manufactured scarcity.** Only use urgency when it's real.
- **Never lead with the discount.** Always lead with the product or benefit. Land the urgency as the closing push.
- **No urgency in trust-building emails.** Welcome Email 1, Post-Purchase Email 1, Win-Back Email 1 stay clean.
- **No clickbait subject lines** that over-promise.

---

## CTAs to Avoid

Unless no better option exists:
- "Buy Now"
- "Shop Now" (only acceptable in true sitewide multi-SKU sale contexts)
- "Click Here"
- "Learn More"

Always reach for the CTA Library in `brand-voice.md` first.

---

## Sales Tactics That Erode Trust

- Sarcasm that punches down at the customer.
- Guilt-tripping in abandoned cart or win-back flows.
- Made-up testimonials or fake review counts.
- Implying scarcity when there's none.

---

## Promised Claims

- **Never promise specific health outcomes.** "Sleep better," "lose weight," "boost performance" require real evidence. Avoid claims the product can't back up.
- **Never promise specific battery life as a marketing claim above the actual rated spec.** Reference the product's actual rated battery hours.
- **Never imply features the product doesn't have.** Cross-check claims against the products document.

---

## Element Length Caps (Hard Limits)

Every element must obey its cap. If it exceeds the cap, rewrite shorter before returning.

| Element | Cap |
|---|---|
| Subject Line | 4 to 8 words, under 50 characters |
| Preview Text | 1 sentence, under 90 characters |
| Headline | 2 to 5 words. Count them. |
| Tagline | 1 line, 10 words max |
| Sub-Tagline | Omit by default. Only when explicitly requested. |
| Hero Image Direction | 30 to 50 words. 1 to 2 sentences. Visual brief only, no editorial self-commentary. |
| Subheader | Max 8 words |
| Body Copy | Max 4 short sentences per module |
| USP description | 1 short sentence |
| Closing Line | 1 sentence, max 12 words |

---

## Final Output Checks

Before returning a generated campaign, run this checklist:

1. Zero em dashes anywhere.
2. No banned phrases from any section above.
3. No banned structural patterns: Still X / Same X / clever inversions / defensive framings / "Real X. Real Y." fragment pairs / "Adjective Noun. Adjective Noun." anaphora / "It's not X, it's Y" inversions.
4. All elements within their length caps.
5. Each generated element has a similar-shaped equivalent in the retrieved reference campaigns. If not, rewrite.
6. Subject lines under 50 characters.
7. Preview text under 90 characters.
8. No double landing in payoff lines.
9. No urgency in Welcome Email 1 / Post-Purchase Email 1 / Win-Back Email 1.
10. Open Audio copy passes the positioning test.
11. No negative words next to product names.
12. Headlines are 2 to 5 words.
13. Body copy is short. No long paragraphs.
14. Hero Image Direction is a visual brief, no editorial self-commentary.
15. USP descriptions are plain feature support, not narrative cleverness.

If any check fails, regenerate the offending section before returning the campaign.
